You've booked the flight. Somewhere between the excitement and the group chat full of advice, a quieter worry shows up: *am I forgetting something?* A visa that won't arrive in time. A card that gets declined at a Dubai checkout. A SIM situation you don't understand until you've already landed.

Here's the good news. A first trip abroad from India isn't complicated — it's just a handful of decisions that nobody laid out for you in order. This guide does that. Sort these six things and you'll land calm, connected, and not overpaying.

We'll keep one rule throughout: for anything that changes — visa rules, fees, processing times — we give you the realistic range and point you to the official source. We won't pretend a number is fixed when it isn't.

## Sort the visa first — everything else waits on it

This is task number one, and it's the one people leave till last. Don't.

Whether you even need a visa depends entirely on your destination. Thailand currently lets Indian passport holders in visa-free for short stays; the UAE does not — most Indians need a tourist visa arranged before they fly. The rules also shift, so the honest move is to check the official portal rather than trust a forum post from 2023.

If you're headed to the Gulf, we've written the specifics in [do Indians need a visa for Dubai](/guides/first-international-trip/dubai-visa-for-indians) — including the residence-permit exception that lets some people skip the pre-arranged visa.

Two things that trip people up regardless of destination:

- **Passport validity.** Most countries want at least six months left on your passport from your travel date, plus a couple of blank pages. If yours is close to expiring, renew it *before* you apply for the visa — a renewal can take weeks.
- **Document mismatches.** A blurry passport scan, a photo with the wrong background, or a hotel booking that doesn't match your dates is the most common reason a straightforward visa gets bounced.

## Your money: the card to leave at home, and the one to carry

Here's the bit that quietly costs first-timers the most.

Your regular Indian debit card works abroad — and that's exactly the problem. Most banks add a forex markup of around 3 to 3.5 percent on every foreign swipe, plus a flat fee per ATM withdrawal, plus GST on the markup. None of it shows up as a line item. You just get home to a statement that's bigger than it should be.

A zero-markup forex or travel card fixes most of this: you load rupees, spend in the local currency at close to the real rate, and freeze the card from an app if it goes missing. On a ₹50,000 trip the difference is usually a couple of thousand rupees — small, but it's money you'd rather keep. We break down the actual maths in [forex card vs credit card abroad](/guides/first-international-trip/forex-card-vs-credit-card).

Cash still matters, just less than you'd think. You need some for taxis, tips, and the odd market stall — but carrying too much is its own risk. How much is "enough" depends on the country and how long you're staying; we put rough per-day numbers in [how much cash to carry on your first trip](/guides/first-international-trip/how-much-cash-to-carry).

## Staying connected without the bill shock

Your carrier's international roaming pack is the expensive default, and it's the one most first-timers panic-buy at the airport.

A travel eSIM is almost always cheaper for data, installs before you fly, and switches on the moment you land — no hunting for a SIM counter after a red-eye. The catch is knowing when roaming actually wins (it sometimes does, for very short trips where you mostly need your Indian number live for OTPs). We compare them properly in [eSIM vs international roaming](/guides/first-international-trip/esim-vs-roaming).

One Dubai-specific quirk worth flagging now: the UAE blocks WhatsApp and FaceTime *voice and video* calls on local networks. On an international travel eSIM, WhatsApp video to home usually still works — which is a real, practical reason to prefer one there.

## Documents — what actually needs to be in your bag

Keep these in your hand luggage, not your checked bag:

- Passport (with that six-month validity)
- Printed return ticket and hotel booking — immigration sometimes asks
- Your visa, printed or saved offline
- A photo of your passport and visa stored separately, in case the originals go missing
- Some local cash and your forex card

That's genuinely it. The pile of photocopies your relatives insist on is mostly for your own peace of mind.

## The airport, step by step

If you've only ever flown domestic, the international airport feels like more — but it's the same few steps, just with an immigration counter added.

You'll check in and drop bags, clear security, then clear *emigration* (an Indian officer stamps you out of the country). You wait at your gate, board, and fly. On the other side you clear *immigration* (their officer stamps you in — have your hotel address and return ticket ready), collect your bags, and walk through customs. That's the whole thing.

Give yourself three hours before an international flight the first time. You'll likely breeze through and have time for a coffee — which beats the alternative.

## A rough timeline

Working backwards from your flight, here's the shape of it:

- **About 3–4 weeks out:** check passport validity, start the visa, sort travel insurance.
- **About 2 weeks out:** order your forex card so the physical card arrives in time.
- **The final week:** install your eSIM and the apps you'll need, withdraw a little foreign cash, screenshot your bookings.
- **The last 48 hours:** pack the documents above in your hand luggage and relax.
