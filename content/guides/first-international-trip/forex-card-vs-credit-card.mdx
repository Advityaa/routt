If you spend ₹1,00,000 abroad on a normal Indian card, the markup alone is roughly ₹3,500 — before GST, before ATM fees. That's the rookie tax, and almost nobody notices it because it never appears as a line on the bill.

Here's how the two cards actually compare, and a quick way to estimate the difference for your own trip.

## What each one costs you

**A normal credit or debit card** adds a foreign-currency markup of around **3 to 3.5 percent** on every overseas swipe. On top of that, GST applies to the markup fee, and ATM withdrawals usually carry a flat fee of a few hundred rupees each. None of it is hidden maliciously — it's just buried.

**A zero-markup forex card** is built for exactly this. You load rupees as the destination currency, spend at close to the real rate with no markup, and lock in your rate when you load. ATM withdrawals are typically a small flat fee rather than a percentage.

## When a credit card still makes sense

The forex card isn't always the only card you carry. A credit card earns its place for:

- **Rewards**, if your card has a genuinely good travel rate that offsets the markup.
- **A backup**, in case the forex card has an issue.
- **Big refundable bookings** and emergencies, where credit-card protections matter.

The sensible first-timer setup is usually a zero-markup forex card for day-to-day spending, a bit of [local cash for the small stuff](/guides/first-international-trip/how-much-cash-to-carry), and a credit card tucked away as backup.

## The takeaway

For everyday foreign spending, a zero-markup forex card almost always beats swiping your regular Indian card — and the gap is bigger the more you spend. Whatever you choose, check the current terms before you load; fees move.
